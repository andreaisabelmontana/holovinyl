HoloVinyl

Hold a record up to the camera and it stops being a flat sleeve — artwork lifts off it, the disc spins, visuals pulse in time. HoloVinyl recognises the album through the lens and overlays an interactive experience on top of it. I rebuilt it from scratch because it's augmented reality grounded in real computer vision: track the object first, then make magic on it.

What it is

A record sleeve that comes alive

The illusion is simple to describe and hard to earn: anything drawn on top of the record has to stick to it perfectly as it moves, tilts and rotates. If the overlay slides even slightly, the magic breaks. So before any visual flourish, HoloVinyl has to solve a pure vision problem — find the album in the frame and work out exactly how it's positioned in space.

Once that pose is known, the album cover becomes a canvas. The system can spin the disc, animate the artwork, or react to the music — all anchored to the physical object the camera is looking at. It's the same pipeline behind every convincing AR effect, built around a thing you can actually hold.

Architecture

How the magic stays glued on

Every frame repeats the recognise-track-render cycle:

  1. Capture

    Read the latest frame from the camera.

  2. Find the cover

    Match features to locate the album and confirm which record it is.

  3. Solve the pose

    Compute the homography and 3D pose of the sleeve in the frame.

  4. Render

    Warp the interactive visuals onto the cover's surface in correct perspective.

  5. Loop

    Repeat fast enough that the overlay tracks the record without visible lag.

Reflection

What rebuilding it taught me

  • AR is a tracking problem wearing a costume. The visuals are the easy part; the believable part is knowing exactly where the object is, every frame.
  • Homographies are the bridge. One matrix maps a known flat image to its place in the scene — and unlocks the entire overlay.
  • Jitter kills immersion. A pose that wobbles frame to frame reads as fake instantly; smoothing the tracking is half the craft.
  • Feature matching has to be robust. Glare, motion blur and partial occlusion are where naive recognition falls apart.
  • Latency is the experience. A correct overlay that arrives a beat late still feels broken — real-time isn't optional here.
